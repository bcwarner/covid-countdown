How reliable are these predictions?

“Hindsight is forever 20/20, but foresight is legally blind” - Ben Graham

The correlation coefficients included are high and indicate some degree of correctness, but in reality are significantly off. There are several reasons to doubt these estimates, including that there are a limited number of points, and the model takes a very limited amount of information into consideration. This model attempts to predict when everyone will receive their first dose, from which the target date is provided. The date upon which reopenings occur will be contingent upon reaching herd immunity where you live, and as such, will be determined by your local health officials.

Should I use these predictions in planning?

No. These predictions are merely to provide perspective and educate. Vaccine distribution could slow down significantly, or increase rapidly, and so the actual date that everyone will be vaccinated may differ from this model's prediction. Should you utilize these predictions, it is entirely at your risk.

How are these estimations made?

This model uses sklearn's LinearRegression and attempts to map total vaccinations (1st dose) to date. The regression may be taken upon the idenity (i.e. unaltered value), the natural log, or the square root of the vaccinations. It attempts to find a fit that ideally minimizes the error between the best fit linear equation and the ground truth. The dataset used is provided by Our World in Data, which is in aggregated from various sources, one of which is the U.S. CDC. The U.S. CDC data is ultimately what is regressed upon.

How can I get vaccinated?

Please refer to your local public health officials for when you can get vaccinated.

Is the vaccine safe/effective?

Yes, the vaccines currently available in the U.S. must follow guidelines set out by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, which outline safety and efficacy. The two current vaccines available in the U.S., designed and produced by Pfizer/BioNTech and Moderna, have a demonstrated 95% efficacy and 94.1% efficacy, respectively. These numbers assume that your doses are administered under the conditions that were tested in these trials; if you ended up receiving the doses differently, the effects may be different.

Can I go back to normal after getting a vaccine?

Not necessarily. While you may be immune to COVID-19 after getting the disease, it is unknown if you are still able to spread it. Because of this, you should still follow preventative measures (social distancing, masking, etc.) until public health officials in your state determine when it is safe to do so.
